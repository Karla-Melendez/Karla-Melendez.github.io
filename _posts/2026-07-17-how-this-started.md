Every project has an origin story. Mine started as a homework assignment.

I was working through the AI Technical Writing Certification with Technical Writer HQ, and one of the early exercises asked us to use an LLM to simulate a user interview: pick a product, invent a persona, and get the model to roleplay a conversation about their experience using the product, including their needs and frustrations. As an aquarist myself, I chose someone using an app to help aquascape their fish tank.

At some point in that invented interview, my fictitious user said an AR feature would be awesome; the ability to point a phone at an empty tank and see how a layout might actually look before buying a single plant or stone.

That single line stuck with me.

## One Assignment Became Five

The certificate kept handing me new formats to practice — a proposal, a user guide, an API guide, a set of product use cases — and each time, instead of picking a new example product, I stayed with the aquascape app. Partly convenience, partly a yearning for such an app myself. By the end of it, I'd written documentation for a product that didn't exist, from enough angles that it started to feel like it should.

So I went looking for the real thing.

## The Gap

I searched for aquascaping apps online, and asked an LLM to help me search too, specifically hunting for anything with AR features. I found a few contenders. None of them had what I'd been imagining across five assignments. That combination of visualization, planning, and guidance built specifically around aquascaping rather than bolted onto a general fishkeeping or aquarium-maintenance app.

So I asked myself the obvious next question: why not build it?

I have a Computing Science degree. I understand how code works, even if I don't have working experience developing software. I'm a technical writer, which means I'm good at figuring out how things should work for the person using them. And I'd already spent a certificate's worth of assignments thinking through this exact product from a dozen angles. With AI as a development partner, building it myself stopped sounding like a stretch and started sounding like a plan.

## Where Things Stand

Right now, Aquascape Vision is in initial planning: figuring out scope, architecture, and what the AR feature actually needs to do versus what would just be nice to have. I'll be using this space to document that process as it happens: decisions made, dead ends hit, things that turn out harder (or easier) than expected.

If you're curious what happens when a technical writing certificate accidentally turns into a product idea, you're in the right place.
