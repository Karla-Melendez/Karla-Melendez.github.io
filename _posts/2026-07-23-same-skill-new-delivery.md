I’ve been a tech writer for 20+ years. In all that time, I’ve witnessed, and been part of, 
the switch from manuals–bound, hard-copy tomes–to online knowledge base articles.

Is one better than the other? I would say they serve different audiences living in different eras. 
Manuals were useful before the Internet became ubiquitous. All the information needed to operate 
a complex software system was stored in a single bound book end-users could place on a shelf, 
pick up when needed, and leaf through to the section that would solve the current problem faced by the end-user.

But when the Internet became easily accessible to everyone, and software companies could store their knowledge bases 
online for end-users to access anywhere in the world, it made more sense to create articles themed around specific uses 
or features of complex software systems. For this work, though, articles should be self-contained (i.e., each article must 
act as a “page one”). It’s been my experience that busy end-users will not be too keen on clicking previous or next to 
paginate through articles. Their online search might also lead them to the middle of a set of articles, and if the article 
itself doesn’t have a clear navigation method, the information on the page will be incomplete, resulting in user frustration.

With the advent of LLMs and AIs, we’re witnessing a new shift: online knowledge base articles must not only be self-contained, 
but they must also be searchable by both human and bot audiences. What this means for tech writers is that the content 
they write should not only include SEO keywords, but should also be parsable by LLMs and chatbots so they can serve up 
the relevant content to the humans requesting the information they need to solve their particular problem.

And yet, regardless of the delivery method, the skill set required to be an effective tech writer remains the same. 
Content must be written clearly, concisely, objectively (i.e., no marketing-speak), and with an eye to specific 
user personas–novice, intermediate, advanced.
